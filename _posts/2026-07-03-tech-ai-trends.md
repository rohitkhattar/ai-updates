## 1. OpenAI floats handing Washington a 5% stake in the company
Sam Altman has pitched the Trump administration on giving the U.S. government a 5% equity stake in OpenAI — a slice worth roughly $42.6 billion based on the company's $852 billion March valuation — as part of a broader plan under which leading AI labs would cede similar stakes to a government vehicle. The pitch, first reported by the Financial Times and now the subject of wall-to-wall coverage, has been raised directly with President Trump, Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick, and Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent — a striking level of access for what's still described as an early-stage, conceptual discussion. Altman has floated the idea for over a year, framing it as the best way to "share the upside of AI" with the public, and has previously proposed a "public wealth fund" to distribute AI-driven economic gains more broadly. The precedent people keep citing is the government's 10% stake in Intel, taken last August after an $8.9 billion investment — Trump has reportedly called the AI-stake idea "a partnership with the American public." None of the other major labs — Anthropic, Google, Meta — have signaled any appetite for a similar arrangement, and it's unclear whether Congress or antitrust regulators would even permit it. For builders, it's a signal that frontier AI is now squarely a matter of state, not just private capital — expect more of the industry's biggest decisions to run through Washington from here.

## 2. Meta's cloud-reselling pivot splits Wall Street and torches the neocloud trade

It's the clearest flashpoint yet in the broader "AI bubble" debate that's dominated markets this week, following South Korea's chip-stock circuit-breaker crash on the same worry. ## 5. The Fed's new headache: a $700B AI spending blitz that looks inflationary
A fresh survey from the National Association for Business Economics finds 80% of forecasters now expect the AI buildout to push inflation higher, complicating the case for rate cuts just as roughly $700 billion in AI-related capex works its way through the U.S. economy. The mechanism is straightforward: massive, concentrated demand for chips, power, land, and skilled labor is bidding up prices across adjacent sectors — electricity, construction, specialized components — even where AI itself isn't directly involved. It's a notable reversal from the earlier narrative that AI would be broadly disinflationary by boosting productivity; instead, the sheer scale and speed of the infrastructure spend is acting like a classic demand shock in a handful of tight markets. Fed officials now have to weigh a labor market that's cooling in AI-exposed white-collar roles against an investment boom that's pushing prices up elsewhere — a genuinely awkward mix for setting policy. It's a good reminder that "AI is deflationary" was always an oversimplification, and the second-order effects of the buildout itself are becoming a macro story in their own right.

## 6. Microsoft and Amazon are sending armies of engineers to sit inside customer companies
Bloomberg's newsletter today zeroes in on a fast-growing trend: both Microsoft and Amazon are now embedding "forward-deployed engineers" (FDEs) directly with enterprise customers to co-build agentic AI solutions, borrowing a playbook popularized by Palantir and more recently OpenAI and Anthropic. It's also a tacit admission that generic platform plays are hitting a ceiling: the cloud giants are now competing on services-heavy, high-touch delivery rather than pure infrastructure. For consulting and systems-integration shops, this is existential — the hyperscalers are moving onto their turf with deep pockets and their own models baked in.

## 7. AWS gives cleared defense contractors a classified cloud, Northrop Grumman first in
AWS has launched Secret Cloud for Industry (ASCI), letting cleared U.S. defense contractors and research institutions in the National Industrial Security Program run classified workloads — up to the Secret level — directly on AWS infrastructure instead of building their own on-prem environments from scratch. Northrop Grumman is the first customer live on the service; without it, the company says its initial workload in AWS's Secret-East region would have required months of hardware procurement before a single job could run. AWS is backing adoption with up to $20 million in credits through an ASCI Accelerator Initiative aimed at defense-industrial-base companies, federally funded research centers, and systems integrators, replacing capital-intensive classified-infrastructure buildouts with pay-as-you-go pricing. It's a meaningful expansion of the government-cloud land grab that's been building since AWS's original GovCloud push, now explicitly targeting the contractor ecosystem rather than agencies themselves. Combined with the $1 billion FDE investment, it's a clear signal AWS sees public-sector and defense-adjacent AI workloads as one of the biggest growth vectors left in cloud.
