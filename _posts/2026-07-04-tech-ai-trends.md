## 7. "Loop engineering" becomes the coding-agent world's hottest new buzzword
A new term is spreading fast through the AI coding community this week: "loop engineering," popularized by Chrome/web-perf veteran Addy Osmani after both Peter Steinberger (creator of OpenClaw, who recently joined OpenAI) and Anthropic's Boris Cherny (creator and head of Claude Code) independently made the same point — stop prompting agents one message at a time, and instead design the loop that prompts them. Cherny put it bluntly on stage: "I don't prompt Claude anymore... I have loops running. They're the ones prompting Claude and figuring out what to do. My job is to write loops." The idea: define a recursive goal, give the agent a way to discover work, act on it, verify the result, persist state, and know when to hand back to a human — a system, not a single instruction — which is exactly the shift powering scheduled/autonomous agent workflows now shipping across coding tools. It's a genuine vocabulary shift for how practitioners think about agentic engineering, on par with how "prompt engineering" itself became a discipline in 2023 — and it's already spawning its own tooling (loop-audit, loop-init, loop-cost utilities on GitHub) within days of the term catching on. For anyone building with coding agents day to day, this is a useful reframe: the leverage isn't in a cleverer prompt, it's in the harness that runs, checks, and re-runs the agent without you in the loop.
